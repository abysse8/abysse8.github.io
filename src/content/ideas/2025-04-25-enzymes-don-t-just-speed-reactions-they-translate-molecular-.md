When I asked what the deepest problem the body faces in enzyme catalysis is, I wanted something past the textbook answer of 'lowering activation energy.' The real answer I landed on: the hard part isn't speed, it's regulated catalysis — maintaining spatially and temporally controlled enzyme activity that faithfully reflects the cell's informational needs, sustainably and robustly against noise. Enzymes only work because gradients exist, but enzymes are also what maintain those gradients, so the whole system is beautifully self-referential and fragile. The deepest framing treats this as an information-theory problem as much as a chemistry one: converting multi-dimensional signals into a molecular ON/OFF switch using only shapes and tiny energy differences. What's open is whether that 'catalysis as information processing' lens actually buys predictive power or is just a satisfying metaphor.
