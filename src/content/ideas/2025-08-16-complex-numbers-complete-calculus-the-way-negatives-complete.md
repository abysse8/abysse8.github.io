I think the significance of complex analysis for single-variable calculus can be explained by analogy to why negative numbers matter in elementary algebra — not by way of multivariable calculus. Negative numbers resolve holes in arithmetic (subtraction always works); complex numbers resolve analytic holes (contour integrals, singularities, analytic continuation). The key is that $\mathbb{C}\cong\mathbb{R}^2$ is the sweet spot: two components give you a full commutative field where multiplication encodes rotation and scaling, so differentiability becomes rigid (Cauchy–Riemann locks two real functions into a harmonic partnership). That rigidity is why holomorphic implies infinitely differentiable, and why the residue theorem reads as a global topological symmetry. Higher dimensions (quaternions, octonions) lose commutativity or field structure, so the miracle doesn't carry over.
